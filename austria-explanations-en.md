## Explanations

## I. General part

The Gambling Act provides for a federal gambling monopoly and thus a limited and strictly supervised legal gambling offer. This serves to safeguard objectives in the general interest, such as ensuring high standards of player protection, avoiding criminal acts, including money laundering and terrorist financing, avoiding addiction and the economic threat to the existence of individuals, and protecting minors. The regulatory framework helps to ensure that concessionaires and licence holders are able to provide the games of chance assigned to them as a reliable and attractive alternative in accordance with the requirements of Union law. This is intended to prevent gamblers from drifting into illegal and uncontrolled gambling offers.

### Reregulation of online gambling

In order to counter the problem of a growing illegal offer, a new regulation of the online gambling market in Austria is being carried out according to the model of international best practice examples. The reform will create a strictly regulated licensing system for online gambling. Under the new conditions, several providers in Austria will be able to offer online gambling in the future. This amendment is intended to create an attractive online gambling offer that is intended to ensure a high level of channelling into the legal market and the highest possible standard of player protection.

A licence is only open to those companies that meet the high legal requirements and follow the strict player protection regulations. In addition, only those companies are admitted that fully fulfil their tax obligations both for the past and for the future. A further prerequisite for the granting of a licence is that all performance judgments imposed by Austrian courts against aggrieved players ("player protection actions") are settled.

Based on practical experience, a package of measures is to be adopted to tighten enforcement against illegal and thus unregulated gambling. In particular, providers of online gambling who do not have an Austrian licence undermine the efforts of the Austrian gambling regulation for a high level of player protection. The authorities are to be provided with supervisory instruments, such as test matches and specific supervisory procedures against illegal online providers, in order to protect the legal and well-regulated market. For example, illegal online providers are to be listed on so-called blacklists. This measure is intended to help educate players about harmful offers and to push back online gambling offers that are not permitted in Austria.

The newly regulated online market is flanked by a special blocking order procedure against providers of information society services ("network blocking") and with provisions on payment blocking. These measures are intended to make access to illegal online gambling offers drastically more difficult and to effectively prevent payment flows to and from illegal online providers.

The suppression of illegal slot machine gambling in Austria has shown that the enforcement of gambling regulation requires enormous efforts, but at the same time is possible and important even in a difficult environment in order to create legal certainty and credibility. The present bundle of different measures is intended to strengthen legal online gambling and intensify the suppression of illegal offers.

### Further development of player protection

In addition to the new regulation of online gambling, the further development and raising of player protection standards in accordance with current scientific findings is another focus of the reform. The focus of the legal changes is on player-related measures. Targeted additions to the existing player protection system are intended to create a stable and attractive gambling offer in which the consumer is the focus.

For example, a mandatory, cross-operator and cross-game blocking register is to be introduced, which will be designed to be open to technology and tamper-proof. Thus, in the future, both operator-side blocks and self-locks will be recorded centrally. In this way, players who are particularly at risk of addiction can be effectively protected.

At the same time, the obligations and liability provisions to ensure player protection for providers will be extended to online gambling. This is also intended to take into account the case law of the Constitutional Court, as most recently in the judgment of 14 December 2022, G 259/2022, by prescribing the legal obligation to monitor problematic gambling behaviour with the possibility of taking legal action before civil courts for all types of games of chance (player protection monitoring).

As a further measure, effective deposit limits for online gambling and for national draws with gambling machines will be introduced. The legal restriction of the maximum deposit amounts is intended to provide a further protective measure that provides special protection for young adults.

In the online area, an online gambling supervisory system will also be implemented, with which the legally regulated deposit limits will be centrally monitored with a cross-operator limit register. In individual cases (for adults who have reached the age of 23), the deposit limit can be increased if the players have a sufficient credit rating. However, the increase in the deposit limit must be accompanied by close player monitoring in order to continue to ensure effective player protection.

In addition to the legally binding restrictions, the strengthening of personal responsibility is also to be promoted. With the help of the mandatory setting of time and monetary limits for state lotteries with gambling machines and online gambling by players themselves, players are to be offered a legally secured opportunity for self-limitation. The increase in the self-imposed limits is subject to a waiting period of 72 hours in order to prevent impulsive behaviour and strengthen self-control.

In order to better assess the concrete danger of gambling, a legal obligation to submit gambling addiction potential analyses is to be introduced. Furthermore, the legally provided responsible standard for gambling advertising according to a scientific standard is to be specified by ordinance so that the advertising restrictions can be further developed in a targeted manner and new phenomena can be reacted to quickly by supervisory law.

With regard to the offer of gambling machines, which – after sports betting – are burdened with the highest prevalence of addiction, existing restrictions are to be tightened. In addition to slowing down the game speed to two seconds, a mandatory cool-down period after 90 minutes of play is to be introduced, during which the player is specifically informed about the risks of gambling with videos. The maximum permissible stake is also to be reduced to 5 euros per game.

The new regulation is intended to ensure a clearly regulated, monitored offer that meets the highest standards of player protection and responsible gambling. In the future, limits for virtual gaming machines (slots in online gambling) are also to be introduced. The restriction of the mandatory maximum stakes and maximum winnings, as well as the limitation of the speed of play and the obligation of a cooling-off period for players, will thus be extended to the online sector. This ensures a high standard of player protection in online gambling, analogous to terrestrial slot machine gambling.

The package of measures for player protection is ambitious and places high demands on gambling providers in Austria. It underlines the high priority of player protection and is intended to create nothing less than the basis for the social acceptance of strict gambling regulation.

### Competence:

The competence of the Federation to enact this Federal Act results from Article 10.1 no. 4 of the Federal Constitution Act (Federal Finance and Monopolies) and from § 7.1 and 7.2 of the F-VG 1948.

### Re § 5 para. 4 nos. 8 to 11, § 5 para. 5 nos. 1 to 3 and 6, § 16 para. 15, § 26 para. 3 (gambling addiction potential analyses, information on gambling behaviour, stake and win limits, cooling-off phase):

This amendment is intended to extend the obligation for federal licence holders to carry out analyses of the potential for gambling addiction, some of which are subject to notices, to the entire gambling sector.

Depending on the design, games of chance have a varying risk of developing problematic or pathological gambling behaviour. In order to effectively safeguard player protection and to prevent gambling addiction in a targeted manner, a risk-based regulatory approach is to be enshrined in law.

The aim of the proposed regulation is to oblige providers of games of chance to carry out structured analyses of the potential for gambling addiction. These analyses are intended to systematically evaluate the respective offer and the associated advertising and to better present the concrete risk of addictive gambling behaviour.

The possibility of inspecting one's own gambling behaviour is intended to contribute to a correct self-assessment of one's own gambling behaviour ("informed player") and thus further improve player protection.

The interval for the mandatory cool-down period will be reduced to 90 minutes and the duration will be extended to 15 minutes and will be intended to interrupt the course of the game to protect players and thus promote responsible gambling.

As further building blocks to improve player protection, the maximum permissible stake per game will be reduced to 5 euros and the minimum playing time will be increased to 2 seconds. This slows down the frequency of play and is intended to reduce game losses. In order to maintain the attractiveness of the game in terms of the intended channelling, jackpots are now permitted.

The use of video content that warns of the risks of gambling is also intended to protect players, as graphic warnings cause a higher perceived danger as well as greater personal concern compared to pure text warnings, and videos are more likely to cause a higher level of personal concern.

The realignment is intended to determine that the right to conduct online gambling (§ 13) is no longer to be tied to the operation of certain lotteries (§§ 6 to 12a) and that the lottery and online gambling market, which was previously organised by granting an individual licence, is to be reorganised. The aim is to adapt the legal framework to the actual developments in the digital market and to ensure effective and up-to-date regulation.

According to the established case law of the Court of Justice of the European Union, it is permissible under EU law to provide for different models in a regulatory system for games of chance, such as a state monopoly for certain games and a licensing system for others (see, inter alia, ECJ judgment of 8 September 2010, Carmen Media Group, C-46/08). Consequently, this also applies to a regulatory system which, as in the present case, provides for a single licence for lotteries and a limited number of licences for casinos as well as an unlimited number of licences in the online sector, i.e. for a regulatory system that is now common in this or a similar form in almost all of Europe. The mere coexistence of such rules does not affect the justification of a monopoly in pursuit of legitimate objectives, especially since the regulation of online gambling offers is geared towards the same objectives as the regulation of terrestrial games of chance.

### Re § 14 (4) and § 16 (14) (distribution):

With the amendment, the term **"tobacco wear business"**, which originated from the Tobacco Monopoly Act 1968 (TabMG 1968) and is no longer in use, is adapted to the terminology of the Tobacco Monopoly Act 1996 (TabMG 1996).

The provision of § 16.14 provides that tobacconists are primarily used for the sale of lottery products, whereby the proven preference for tobacconists is to be retained for socio-political reasons. This adaptation is intended to ensure that tobacco shops in particular remain the primary terrestrial distribution channel for lottery products in the future.

In the licence decision, the gambling supervisory authority must define a distribution structure for an overall effective distribution network in Austria. In principle, it can be assumed that the existing lottery distribution network will contribute to an effective and secure supply of lottery products to the population and that a similar sales structure will therefore be maintained in the future. The provision therefore does not preclude the possibility that other acceptance points will continue to exist in addition to tobacconists. In particular, acceptance points in medium-sized trading companies (e.g. independent merchants, petrol stations) and post offices are to be retained in addition to tobacconists.

In addition, there should be a limited possibility of switching to other sales outlets which, due to their location, are suitable for contributing to an effective and nationwide distribution network. Such a departure from the primary distribution channel via tobacconists provided for by law must be examined by the gambling supervisory authority on the basis of the criteria provided for in the law.

The provisions on the drafting of contracts are intended to take account of the concessionaire's negotiating position and to contribute to reasonable conditions of distribution. In particular, it is to be ensured that established contract contents and remuneration levels continue to contribute to effective terrestrial distribution of lottery products in the future.
